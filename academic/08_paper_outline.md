# Paper Outline

Two outline options. Decision pending Sarkar conversation (see `00_response_to_sarkar.md`, §4).

## Option A — "Capability index as critical instrument" (default)

**Working title.** Reading Digital Public Infrastructure Through the Capability Approach: A Critical-Instrument Analysis of Available Cross-National Indicators.

**Word target.** 9000–10000 words excluding appendices. Target venue: *Information, Communication & Society* (Q1, IF ~7).

**Structure.**

| § | Title | ~Words | Argument |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Introduction | 800 | The DPI moment has produced a flood of digital-development indices. Few are capability-grounded. The paper asks: what does the standard indicator family see, what does it miss, and what would a capability-grounded reading reveal? |
| 2 | Background | 1500 | Capability approach (Sen, Nussbaum, Robeyns); digital-divide literature (Helsper, Hilbert, Robinson); critical data studies (Merry, Bhuta et al., Couldry & Mejias); DPI critique (Khera, Masiero, Krishna); existing indices (IDI, NRI, EGDI, GTMI, BCG, World Bank DPI Composite). Position the paper. |
| 3 | A capability-grounded scheme for digital indicators | 1200 | The four-layer scheme (resources / conversion factors / capabilities / functionings) as a diagnostic, not a new index. Six-domain redefinition. |
| 4 | Method | 1500 | Source set; gating rules; construct-validity audit procedure; quantitative composite (DCLO) only as a comparator instrument; identification strategy for the limited causal layer; robustness protocol. |
| 5 | Findings I — what the standard indicator family captures and misses | 1500 | Indicator-validity audit results. The "service enablement" and "outcome" domains are macro-economic, not capability. Construct error, not coverage error. Comparison with IDI, EGDI, GTMI. |
| 6 | Findings II — when DCLO disagrees with the rank order, the rank order wins | 1200 | Method-comparison instability (`data/gold/dclo_method_comparison.csv`). The fact that the causal-signal score and the index disagree across years is itself the finding. Capability-targeted re-construction proposed. |
| 7 | Findings III — case studies | 1200 | India (UPI 2016 event-study); Estonia (e-residency 2014); Brazil (PIX 2020). What the standard index sees vs what the capability re-reading sees. |
| 8 | Discussion | 800 | The index as critical instrument. Implications for DPI policy: rapid build-out without capability grounding can produce high index scores and persistent capability gaps. |
| 9 | Limitations | 400 | Listed honestly; pre-registration acknowledged; data-coverage caveats. |
| 10 | Conclusion | 300 | The paper does not propose to replace the index family but to read it differently. The dashboard is the artefact through which the reading is made operational. |

| App. B | Robustness protocol | — | Full table from `05_robustness_protocol.md`. |

## Option B — "Political economy of DPI through a capability lens" (Sarkar-leaning alternative)

**Working title.** What the Index Cannot See: A Capability Reading of Digital Public Infrastructure in India.

**Word target.** 11000–12000 words. Target venue: *Big Data & Society* (Q1, IF ~8) or *Development and Change* (Q1, IF ~3.5).

**Structure.**

| § | Title | ~Words | Argument |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Introduction | 1000 | DPI as a political-economy project; the Indian state's narrative; the index family's role in legitimating it. |
| 2 | The DPI critique | 1800 | Khera, Masiero, Rao, Krishna, Bhuta et al., Couldry & Mejias. What the critique already establishes. |
| 3 | The capability approach as a counter-frame | 1500 | Sen, Nussbaum, Robeyns. Why capability is the right frame for the critique. |
| 4 | The indicator family and its silences | 2000 | Indicator-validity audit applied to country and India-state tracks; case studies of inclusionary failure (Aadhaar denial-of-service; gendered exclusion in UPI). |
| 5 | An alternative reading of the rankings | 2000 | DCLO as a critical instrument; rank-order disagreement; the case for capability-targeted indicators. |
| 6 | India case studies | 1800 | Three Indian states (one high-rank, one middle, one low) read through the capability scheme; what the rank says, what the on-the-ground capability gap shows. |
| 7 | Discussion and policy implications | 800 | Whose questions does the index answer; what would change if it answered different ones. |
| 8 | Conclusion | 400 | — |
